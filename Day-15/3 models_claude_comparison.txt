"Summarise this patient note and flag any clinical or documentation risks: 'Pt c/o chest pain ×2 days. Vitals stable. EKG done - unremarkable. Discharged home with ibuprofen and advised to
follow up if symptoms worsen. No troponin ordered. Attending noted time pressure due to ED volume."


Patient Summary
A patient with 2 days of chest pain was evaluated in the ED with stable vital signs, an unremarkable EKG, and was sent home with ibuprofen. No troponin test was ordered.


HAIKU:


CRITICAL CLINICAL RISKS
1. MISSING CARDIAC MARKER TEST (Troponin)
Why this is dangerous:

Troponin is the gold standard blood test to detect if the heart muscle has been damaged
An EKG can appear normal even when there IS heart damage
Chest pain + no troponin = incomplete evaluation
Patient could have a heart attack at home and no one caught it

2. PREMATURE DISCHARGE
What went wrong:

Patient was sent home without ruling out serious causes of chest pain
Chest pain lasting 2 days is not trivial
Should have had either:

Serial troponins (drawn at 0, 3, and 6 hours), OR
Admission for observation, OR
Clear cardiology follow-up arranged BEFORE discharge



3. TIME PRESSURE BIAS (COGNITIVE ERROR)
The biggest problem:

Doctor noted "time pressure due to ED volume"
This is a known cause of premature closure - doctors stop investigating when busy
The doctor made a decision quickly without complete workup
This is documented in the note, which is actually dangerous evidence

4. VAGUE DISCHARGE INSTRUCTIONS
Follow-up instruction problem:

"Follow up if symptoms worsen" is unclear
What if pain stays the same or gets slightly better?
Patient might think "it's getting better" and not seek help
Better instruction: "Return immediately for any chest pain, shortness of breath, or sweating"

5. MISSING DOCUMENTATION
What should be documented but isn't:

Why troponin was NOT ordered (no documented reasoning)
Risk stratification (does patient have diabetes, smoking history, family history?)
Specific return precautions



OPUS:
Clinical risks
The biggest concern is that chest pain was not fully worked up before discharge. 
A normal EKG alone does not rule out a heart attack or other serious heart problem. 
The troponin blood test, which detects heart muscle damage, was not ordered, so a real cardiac event could have been missed.

Ibuprofen treats pain and inflammation but does nothing for a blocked artery, 
so giving it could mask symptoms while the underlying problem continues.
The discharge advice — "come back if symptoms worsen" — is too vague. 
Chest pain can stay the same or even ease off while a dangerous condition is still developing, 
and a patient might wrongly decide they're fine.

Documentation risks
Writing down that the decision was made under time pressure due to ED volume is a real liability flag. If this patient later has a bad outcome, that line suggests the workup was rushed rather than based on clinical judgment.
There is also no recorded reasoning for why troponin was skipped, no risk assessment (age, history of heart disease, diabetes, smoking, family history), and no clear safety-netting instructions. Those gaps make the note hard to defend.

This looks like an incomplete chest pain evaluation with weak documentation. 
The safer path would have been a troponin test (often repeated a few hours apart), a 
documented risk assessment, clear return-to-ER instructions, and arranged follow-up before sending the patient home.


SONNET:

Clinical Risks
Missing troponin test — most critical issue

Troponin is a blood test that detects heart muscle damage. It is the standard test for ruling out a heart attack. 
A normal EKG alone is not enough — a patient can have a completely normal EKG and still be having a heart attack. 
Without troponin, this workup is medically incomplete.

Two days of chest pain sent home without full workup

Chest pain lasting 48 hours significantly raises the concern for serious cardiac conditions including unstable angina 
or myocardial infarction. This duration alone typically warrants a thorough cardiac investigation before discharge.

Ibuprofen is the wrong treatment if this is cardiac

Ibuprofen is an anti-inflammatory painkiller. It is not appropriate for cardiac chest pain and can actually increase cardiovascular risk. If the underlying cause is heart-related, this medication could cause harm and mask deteriorating symptoms.
Vague discharge advice

"Follow up if symptoms worsen" gives the patient no clear guidance. Chest pain from a cardiac event can stay steady 
or even temporarily ease while the condition worsens. The patient needs specific warning signs spelled out — 
such as pain spreading to the arm or jaw, breathlessness, sweating, or dizziness.

Documentation Risks
Time pressure recorded as a clinical factor

The attending noted being rushed due to ED volume. This is a significant legal and professional liability. 
It implies the discharge decision was driven by workload rather than clinical safety, which would be very hard 
to defend if the patient deteriorates.

No explanation for skipping troponin

The note does not document why the troponin was not ordered. Without written justification, this reads 
as an omission rather than a deliberate, reasoned clinical choice.
No cardiac risk assessment documented

There is no record of the patient's age, prior heart conditions, diabetes, smoking history, cholesterol levels, 
or family history of heart disease. These are essential factors in any chest pain evaluation and must be documented 
to justify a discharge decision.
No confirmed follow-up

The note says the patient was "advised to follow up" but there is no confirmed appointment, 
referral, or named provider. This does not meet the standard of a safe discharge plan.
